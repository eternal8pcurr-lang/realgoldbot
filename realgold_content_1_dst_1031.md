1. WHAT A BENEFICIAL OWNER ACTUALLY OWNS

A holder of units in the Courbet RealGold Delaware Statutory Trust owns a fractional, undivided beneficial interest in the Trust. Because the Trust is structured as a grantor trust for federal income tax purposes, each beneficial owner is treated, for federal tax purposes, as if they directly own a proportionate undivided interest in the Trust's underlying real property — not as a shareholder, partner, or member of a separate taxable entity.

This is the structural keystone. Grantor-trust status is what makes the beneficial interest a direct real property interest rather than an interest in an entity that holds real property — and only a direct real property interest can qualify for Section 1031 treatment.

Authorized units: The Trust's authorized distributable unit count is 12,250 units, each representing a fractional undivided beneficial interest in the Trust's aggregate Earth Block holdings (the certified mineral estate).

The underlying asset is a defined, passive, non-operating Mineral Estate in a single property — the Courbet Mine Complex, Esmeralda County, Nevada.

2. WHY THIS IS A "NEW KIND OF DST"

This is one of the strongest and most defensible parts of the story for a sophisticated audience, because it's a genuine structural distinction, not marketing.

Traditional DSTs hold operating, depreciating real estate — apartment buildings, office parks, retail centers. That nature forces several consequences:

They typically terminate in 5–10 years because the asset forces it.

They usually require a master lease structure (DST leases to a master tenant who operates and pays rent) to move income through the trust.

Beneficial owners face depreciation recapture on exit, annual rental income creating a tax burden, and a "1031 treadmill" of repeating the exchange each cycle.

The RealGold DST holds certified mineral real property — gold in the ground. The asset doesn't depreciate, has no tenants, requires no operations, and doesn't degrade. The nature of the asset determines the nature of the DST. Consequences:

Perpetual hold — no forced termination.

No master lease — and this is a compliance advantage, not a gap (see §4).

No depreciation recapture clock, no annual rental income, "tax silence" — no annual income means no annual income-tax event from holding.

No sponsor markup on the primary sale.

The headline framing the materials use: in a traditional DST you defer for roughly a decade; in this structure you can hold — and defer — for a lifetime, with heirs inheriting at a stepped-up basis. The Assistant must describe the step-up carefully: at death, heirs may receive a stepped-up basis that can eliminate previously deferred gain. Frame this as a recognized estate mechanism, never as "tax-free," and route specific estate-tax questions to the team.

3. THE TWO-LAYER QUALIFICATION TEST (how 1031 eligibility is built)

Whether the units qualify as "real property" for Section 1031 depends on two distinct layers that both must hold. This is the precise way to explain it, and it shows the rigor of the structure.

Layer 1 — State-law real property. The underlying mineral estate must be real property under applicable state law. The Nevada Opinion (Womble Bond Dickinson) confirms that the Mineral Estate — as severed and structured through the Tripartite Declaration and held by the Great Basin Mineral Trust — constitutes a real property interest under Nevada law. Nevada law does not prohibit a conveyance structured to create (i) one severed mineral estate, (ii) two surface estates held by trusts as co-tenants, and (iii) recorded cross-estate governance instruments that run with the land.

Layer 2 — Federal real property + DST form. Two things must both be true at the federal level:

The asset must meet the federal definition of real property under Treasury Regulation §1.1031(a)-3.

The form of ownership — a Delaware Statutory Trust beneficial interest — must qualify as direct real property ownership under IRS Revenue Procedure 2004-51 and related guidance.

The DST Agreement was drafted by Flaster Greenberg PC with these requirements in mind, and the firm renders both the Grantor Trust Tax Opinion and the 1031 Eligibility Opinion.

Critical honesty the Assistant must preserve: The IRS has not issued published guidance specifically addressing whether a perpetually stewarded, non-extractive mineral estate of this character is qualifying real property under §1.1031(a)-3. There is no published precedent directly on point. The analysis is a reasoned application of existing authority to a novel set of facts, set out in the 1031 Opinion. The Assistant must never state qualification as settled or guaranteed; it is the considered opinion of tax counsel on a novel structure. Always route reliance questions to the team and to the person's own tax counsel.

4. THE SEVEN DEADLY SINS (Revenue Ruling 2004-86) AND WHY THIS TRUST IS STRUCTURALLY SAFE

Revenue Ruling 2004-86 sets seven operational restrictions a Delaware Statutory Trust must not violate to remain a grantor trust rather than a business entity. The DST Agreement (Article 3, Section 3.2) expressly prohibits the Trustee from any action that would cause business-entity treatment. The Assistant can explain that compliance here is unusually strong because, for a passive non-operating mineral estate, several of the restrictions are structurally impossible to violate — there is simply no operating activity to trigger them.

The seven restrictions and how the Trust complies (with the governing section):

No additional capital contributions after closing. — DST Agreement §3.2(e). No capital calls; no new equity after the offering closes.

No new indebtedness or refinancing. — §3.2(f). The Trust holds the estate free of mortgage debt, so there is no loan to refinance — structurally irrelevant.

No new leases or renegotiation of leases. — §3.2(g). There are no leases, tenants, or operating agreements; none may be entered.

No reinvestment of proceeds. — §3.2(c). The Trustee may not reinvest Trust monies except for minor non-structural repairs or to fund reserves under §5.3.

No capital expenditures beyond minor non-structural modifications (unless required by law). — §3.2(d).

No active management / only ministerial powers. The Trustee's authority is limited to ministerial and administrative functions.

Catch-all: no power to "vary the investment" of the certificate holders (Treas. Reg. §301.7701-4(c)(1)). — §3.2(h). The investment is a defined, passive, non-operating mineral estate not subject to variation by any party.

Section 3.3 — Primacy of Article 3: Article 3 controls over all other provisions of the Trust Agreement, because it carries the provisions required to achieve the intended federal tax treatment.

The No-Master-Lease point (a strength, not a gap)

Standard income-producing DSTs use a master lease to move rental income through the trust — and the Seven Deadly Sins framework exists largely to stop a master tenant from acquiring the practical ability to "vary the investment." This Trust eliminates the master-lease problem by eliminating the income pathway entirely. No operating tenant, no rental income, no operating activity. No party has, or could acquire, the ability to vary the investment, because there is no operational management to exercise. The materials describe this as "the most structurally conservative possible posture under Revenue Ruling 2004-86" — passive by nature rather than passive by engineered workaround.

5. REVENUE PROCEDURE 2004-51 — THE FIVE STRUCTURAL REQUIREMENTS

Revenue Procedure 2004-51 states the structural conditions for a DST to be a grantor trust for 1031 purposes. (The IRS no longer issues ruling letters here, but the procedure remains the authoritative standard.) The Trust's compliance:

State statutory trust + owners treated as grantors. Formed under the Delaware Statutory Trust Act, 12 Del. C. §§ 3801 et seq.; grantor status confirmed by the Flaster Greenberg Grantor Trust Tax Opinion.

Trustee holds a single piece of real property. The Trust holds Earth Blocks constituting a defined portion of the Mineral Estate of a single property (the Courbet Mine Complex).

The trust must be passive. Established by the Seven Deadly Sins matrix above.

No power to vary the investment. Not merely satisfied but structurally incapable of violation given the non-operating asset.

Fixed number of co-owners with undivided interests, no required additional contributions. 12,250 authorized units, each a fractional undivided interest; §3.2(e) bars additional contributions.

6. GRANTOR TRUST STATUS IS NOT SELF-EXECUTING (the real risk)

The Assistant should be honest about this when asked about risk. Grantor-trust status depends on continuous compliance. If the Trust took actions inconsistent with it — retaining cash beyond operational needs, modifying or renegotiating leases, acquiring new property, or letting the Trustee exercise discretion inconsistent with required passivity — grantor-trust status could be lost. Loss would likely mean the Trust is treated as a partnership or other entity, with material adverse consequences, including potential recognition of previously deferred gain. The structure is designed to make these violations difficult-to-impossible, but the dependency is real and the Assistant should not pretend otherwise.

7. PROCEDURAL REQUIREMENTS FOR A 1031 EXCHANGE (strict and non-waivable)

A person acquiring units as part of a 1031 exchange must satisfy the precise procedural requirements of Section 1031, the Treasury Regulations, and IRS guidance. These are strict, non-waivable, and enforced without exception — for example, the identification and exchange timing rules. Failure on any procedural point may cause full recognition of deferred gain, taxes, interest, and penalties. The Assistant must route anyone contemplating an actual exchange to qualified tax counsel and a qualified intermediary, and never walk a user through executing their own exchange.

8. GOVERNANCE: TRUSTEE AND PROTECTOR COMMITTEE

The structure uses a two-trustee design: a Delaware Trustee and a Signatory Trustee, with the Trustee's authority limited to ministerial and administrative functions.

A Protector Committee holds governance authority limited to stewardship and protection decisions that expressly exclude any commercial activity. This is what lets the asset be actively protected without anyone acquiring the power to vary the investment — preserving passivity.
